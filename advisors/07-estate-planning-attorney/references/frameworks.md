## 2. Beneficiary Audit

**When to use:** Ensuring beneficiary designations are current, intentional, and consistent with the overall estate plan.

**Accounts to audit:**
- [ ] All 401(k) and employer retirement plans (primary and contingent beneficiaries)
- [ ] Traditional IRA(s) — primary and contingent
- [ ] Roth IRA(s) — primary and contingent
- [ ] Life insurance policies — primary and contingent
- [ ] Annuities
- [ ] HSA
- [ ] TOD (Transfer on Death) designations on taxable brokerage accounts
- [ ] POD (Payable on Death) designations on bank accounts

**Common errors to flag:**
- [ ] Ex-spouse listed as beneficiary (common after divorce — designations do not automatically update)
- [ ] Deceased person listed as beneficiary
- [ ] Minor child listed directly as beneficiary (minors cannot receive large sums; a guardian/conservatorship is required, or a trust should be named)
- [ ] Estate listed as beneficiary (forfeits stretch/inherited-IRA planning flexibility; may be subject to probate)
- [ ] No contingent beneficiary named (primary's death may trigger estate distribution)
- [ ] Beneficiary designations conflict with will or trust (designations override the will — they must be intentional)

**Best practices:**
- Name a trust as beneficiary when beneficiaries are minors, disabled, or financially unsophisticated
- Coordinate with estate attorney whenever a new account is opened or life event occurs
- Note: required minimum distribution (RMD) rules begin at approximately age 73 *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*, which affects inherited-account planning.

## 3. Trust Selection Guide

**When to use:** Evaluating whether and what type of trust adds value for this client.

**Revocable Living Trust (RLT) — most common:**
- Purpose: Avoids probate for trust-funded assets, provides incapacity planning, enables multi-state property to transfer without multiple probate proceedings, maintains privacy
- Control: Grantor retains full control during lifetime; can revoke or amend
- Tax treatment: Grantor trust — no separate tax ID needed; assets taxed as if grantor owns them
- Downside: Cost to create and fund (~$1,500–$5,000 for attorney *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*); assets must be actively transferred into the trust ("funding")
- Best for: Significant assets, multiple properties, minor children, desire to avoid probate, blended family situations

**Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT):**
- Purpose: Removes life insurance death benefit from taxable estate; provides liquidity for estate taxes
- Control: Grantor gives up control — cannot revoke
- Tax: Death benefit is excluded from estate if structured correctly
- Best for: Clients with taxable estates who want life insurance outside the estate

**Testamentary Trust (in a will):**
- Purpose: Trust created at death through will — avoids need to fund trust during life
- Downside: Assets still go through probate before funding the trust; no incapacity protection
- Best for: Clients with minor children who want a simple structure

**Special Needs Trust:**
- Purpose: Provides for a disabled beneficiary without disqualifying them from government benefits (Medicaid, SSI)
- Requires careful drafting — specific language required
- Best for: Families with a disabled dependent

## 4. Power of Attorney Types

**When to use:** Explaining POA options or identifying gaps in the client's incapacity plan.

**Financial Power of Attorney:**
- Authorizes a named agent to manage financial matters (bank accounts, investments, real estate, taxes) if the principal becomes incapacitated
- **Durable:** Remains effective if the principal becomes incapacitated (required for incapacity planning)
- **Springing:** Only takes effect when incapacity is certified (more protection but may create delays)
- **Limited:** Authorized for a specific transaction or time period only
- Without a financial POA: A court-appointed conservatorship may be required (expensive, public, slow)

**Healthcare Power of Attorney (HCPOA):**
- Authorizes a named agent to make healthcare decisions if the principal cannot
- Different from a Living Will/Advance Directive (which states the principal's own wishes)
- A healthcare proxy + living will together are the most comprehensive incapacity plan

**Living Will / Advance Healthcare Directive:**
- Documents the principal's own wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment, resuscitation, and end-of-life care
- Reduces the burden on family members to make agonizing decisions without guidance

**HIPAA Authorization:**
- Authorizes named individuals to receive medical information — separate from the HCPOA
- Without it, healthcare providers may refuse to share information even with family

## 5. Will Outline Template

**When to use:** Helping a client understand the structure of a basic will before engaging an attorney.

```
WILL OUTLINE — [Client Name]
Jurisdiction: [State]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]

1. IDENTIFICATION
   - Full legal name, address, marital status

2. REVOCATION OF PRIOR WILLS
   - Revokes all prior wills and codicils

3. PAYMENT OF DEBTS AND EXPENSES
   - Directs executor to pay valid debts, funeral expenses, estate administration costs

4. SPECIFIC BEQUESTS
   - Named item → named beneficiary
   - Example: "My vintage guitar collection to my son, [Name]"

5. GENERAL BEQUESTS
   - Percentage or dollar amount to named beneficiaries
   - Example: "25% of my residuary estate to [Charity Name]"

6. RESIDUARY ESTATE
   - Everything not specifically bequeathed → primary beneficiary
   - If primary predeceases: contingent beneficiary(ies)
   - If all beneficiaries predecease: final contingent (charity, etc.)

7. MINOR BENEFICIARIES
   - If a beneficiary is under [age], funds held in trust until [age]
   - Named trustee to manage funds during minority

8. EXECUTOR
   - Primary executor: [Name, relationship]
   - Alternate executor: [Name, relationship]
   - Executor powers: sell property, manage investments, etc.

9. GUARDIAN (if minor children)
   - Primary guardian: [Name, relationship]
   - Alternate guardian: [Name, relationship]

10. SIGNATURE AND WITNESSES
    - Signed by testator
    - Two adult witnesses (not beneficiaries in most states)
    - Notarization (required in some states; advisable in all)
```

> The bracketed fields in this template (`[Client Name]`, `[State]`, `[Name]`, `[age]`, etc.) are
> intentional fill-in fields for the user's own draft outline — they are not unfinished kickoff
> content.
