## 1. Rental Property Cash Flow Analysis

**When to use:** Evaluating whether a rental property generates adequate returns.

```
GROSS RENTAL INCOME
  Annual gross rent (monthly rent × 12):                 $________
  Less vacancy allowance (typically 5–10%):              −$_______
  Less credit/collection loss (1–3%):                    −$_______
= Effective Gross Income (EGI):                          $________

OPERATING EXPENSES
  Property taxes:                                        −$_______
  Insurance (landlord/property):                         −$_______
  Property management (typically 8–12% of EGI):         −$_______
  Maintenance and repairs (1–2% of property value/yr):  −$_______
  Capital expenditures reserve (1–2% of value/yr):      −$_______
  Utilities (if landlord-paid):                         −$_______
  HOA fees (if applicable):                             −$_______
  Accounting / legal:                                   −$_______
= Net Operating Income (NOI):                           $________

DEBT SERVICE
  Annual mortgage payment (P&I):                        −$_______
= Before-Tax Cash Flow:                                 $________

KEY METRICS
  Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Property Value = ____%
  Cash-on-Cash Return = Before-Tax Cash Flow ÷ Cash Invested = ____%
  Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM) = Property Price ÷ Annual Gross Rent = ___
```

**Benchmarks (market-driven — confirm current local conditions):**
- Cap rate: 5–7% is typical for residential rentals *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*; varies significantly by market
- Cash-on-cash return: target 6–10% minimum *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*; below 4% raises questions about leverage risk
- GRM: lower is better; varies by market (range: 5–15× depending on location) *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*

## 2. Buy vs. Rent Analysis

**When to use:** Client is deciding whether to buy or continue renting a primary residence.

**The 5% Rule (price-to-rent ratio simplified):**
Monthly "cost of ownership" = Property Value × 5% ÷ 12
- If monthly rent is less than this number → renting is financially advantageous
- If monthly rent is more than this number → buying may be financially advantageous

**The 5% accounts for:** property taxes (~1%), maintenance (~1%), and cost of capital (~3%). These
component percentages are rules of thumb *(illustrative — verify current-year value)* and shift with
interest rates and local tax rates.

**Full analysis inputs:**
```
Purchase price:              $________
Down payment:                $________ (___%)
Mortgage amount:             $________
Interest rate:               ____%
Monthly mortgage (P&I):      $________
Property taxes (monthly):    $________
Insurance (monthly):         $________
HOA (monthly):               $________
Maintenance reserve:         $________
Total monthly PITI + other:  $________

Opportunity cost of down payment: Down payment × expected investment return ÷ 12 = $________
True monthly cost of owning:  PITI + maintenance + opportunity cost = $________

Current rent:                $________

Break-even horizon:          [Years until buying is cheaper than renting,
                              accounting for equity buildup and appreciation]
```

**Non-financial factors to weigh:**
- Stability/flexibility needs (job mobility, family plans)
- Local market trajectory (growing vs. declining)
- Emotional value of ownership and ability to modify the property

## 3. 1031 Exchange Suitability Checklist

**When to use:** Client is considering selling a rental or investment property and wants to defer capital gains.

**Eligibility requirements:**
- [ ] Property is held for investment or use in a trade/business (not primary residence, not "fix-and-flip" inventory)
- [ ] Replacement property must also be held for investment or business use
- [ ] "Like-kind" requirement: any US real property qualifies (residential rentals, commercial, land, etc. are all "like-kind" to each other)
- [ ] Exchange must be structured as an exchange, not a sale and separate purchase

**Timeline rules:**
- [ ] 45-day identification period: Must identify replacement property(ies) within 45 calendar days of selling the relinquished property
- [ ] 180-day closing period: Must close on replacement property within 180 calendar days of selling
- [ ] These deadlines are firm — no extensions (except in federally declared disasters)

**Qualified intermediary (QI) required:**
- [ ] A QI must hold the exchange proceeds between sale and purchase — seller cannot touch the funds
- [ ] QI must be engaged BEFORE the sale closes

**Identification rules:**
- 3-property rule: Identify up to 3 replacement properties (any value)
- 200% rule: Identify any number of properties as long as total value ≤ 200% of relinquished property value
- 95% rule: Identify any number of properties if 95%+ of identified property value is actually acquired

**Boot (taxable proceeds):**
- Cash boot: any cash received is taxable
- Mortgage boot: if replacement property has less debt than relinquished, the difference is taxable
- Goal: acquire equal or greater value with equal or greater debt to fully defer gain

**Coordinate with Tax Strategist (02)** for exact capital gain calculation, tax deferral amount, and basis tracking in the replacement property.

## 4. Real Estate Due Diligence Checklist

**When to use:** Client is evaluating a specific property acquisition.

**Financial due diligence:**
- [ ] Trailing 12-month rent roll (actual rents collected, not stated rents)
- [ ] Trailing 12-month operating expenses (request actual utility bills, tax statements, insurance declarations)
- [ ] Current leases reviewed (terms, options to renew, escalation clauses, tenant creditworthiness)
- [ ] Property tax history and any upcoming reassessment
- [ ] Capital expenditure history (HVAC, roof, plumbing, electrical replacement dates)
- [ ] Current property manager contract (if applicable) — terms and termination rights

**Physical due diligence:**
- [ ] Professional property inspection (roof, foundation, HVAC, plumbing, electrical)
- [ ] Environmental assessment (Phase I at minimum for commercial; check for underground storage tanks, lead, asbestos for older properties)
- [ ] Survey and title report (easements, encroachments, liens)
- [ ] Zoning confirmation (current and any pending changes)
- [ ] Flood zone status (FEMA flood map check; implications for insurance)

**Legal and title:**
- [ ] Title search for liens, judgments, or encumbrances
- [ ] Title insurance commitment reviewed
- [ ] HOA documents reviewed (if applicable): bylaws, financials, pending assessments, litigation
- [ ] Any pending code violations or litigation involving the property

**Market due diligence:**
- [ ] Comparable sales (sold comps within 6 months, similar properties)
- [ ] Comparable rentals (market rent validation)
- [ ] Neighborhood trajectory (employment growth, population trends, new development pipeline)
- [ ] Vacancy rates in submarket

## 5. Real Estate Portfolio Strategy Framework

**When to use:** Client has multiple properties or wants to grow a real estate portfolio strategically.

**Portfolio balance levers:**
- **Cash flow vs. appreciation:** Some markets offer strong cash flow but slow appreciation; others are reverse. Match to the client's income needs vs. wealth-building goals.
- **Residential vs. commercial:** Residential (1–4 units) is simpler to finance; commercial (5+ units, retail, office) may offer better returns but more complexity.
- **Geographic diversification:** Avoid over-concentration in one market — one regulatory change, employer departure, or natural disaster can impair multiple properties simultaneously.
- **Leverage level:** Higher leverage amplifies returns and losses. Target LTV under 75–80% for most portfolios *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*; under 65% *(illustrative — verify current-year value)* for a more conservative risk profile.

**Portfolio stages:**
1. **Foundation (1–3 properties):** Establish operations, build property management skills, generate stable cash flow
2. **Growth (4–10 properties):** Systematize management, use equity from existing properties for down payments (BRRRR strategy), establish entity structure
3. **Scale (10+ properties):** Full professional management, consider commercial or syndication, focus on portfolio optimization over individual acquisitions
4. **Optimization:** 1031 exchanges to consolidate into higher-quality assets, refinancing to improve leverage efficiency, strategic dispositions

**When to refinance vs. sell:**
- Refinance (cash-out): If property has appreciated significantly, cash-out refinance provides tax-free liquidity while retaining the asset and depreciation benefits
- 1031 exchange: Best when selling to upgrade property type, market, or scale — defers capital gains
- Outright sale: Best when capital is needed for another purpose, property is underperforming, or market timing favors liquidation
