## 2. Life Insurance Needs Analysis — DIME Method

**When to use:** Calculating how much life insurance a client needs.

**DIME stands for:** Debt + Income + Mortgage + Education

```
D — DEBT (outstanding liabilities to be paid off)
  Credit card and consumer debt:     $________
  Student loans:                     $________
  Auto loans:                        $________
  Business debt with personal guarantee: $______
  Funeral and final expenses (~$15,000 — illustrative — verify current-year value): $______
  Total Debt:                        $________

I — INCOME (income replacement for dependents)
  Annual income to replace:          $________
  Years of support needed:           ___ years
  (At minimum, support until youngest child is independent)
  Income replacement total (annual × years): $________
  [Note: Use a lower multiple if spouse earns significant income independently]

M — MORTGAGE (pay off the mortgage)
  Remaining mortgage balance:        $________

E — EDUCATION (fund children's education)
  Number of children:                ___
  Estimated education cost per child: $________
  Total education funding:           $________

TOTAL LIFE INSURANCE NEED:
  D + I + M + E =                   $________
  Less: Existing coverage:          −$_______
  Less: Existing liquid savings:    −$_______
  Additional coverage needed:        $________
```

**Term vs. permanent guidance:**
- **Term life:** Covers a defined period (10, 20, 30 years). Lower cost. Best for income replacement during working years and while debt/dependents exist.
- **Whole life:** Permanent coverage + cash value accumulation. Higher cost. Best for estate liquidity, business buy-sell funding, or permanent need.
- **Universal life:** Flexible permanent coverage. Variable cost and benefit. More complex.

For most clients with dependents and a mortgage, term life covering 20+ years is the most cost-effective solution.

## 3. Disability Insurance Evaluation

**When to use:** Assessing whether a client has adequate income protection in the event of disability.

**Why it matters:** The probability of becoming disabled during working years significantly exceeds the probability of dying during those same years. Yet most clients are underinsured for disability.

**Coverage analysis:**
```
Client's monthly gross income:                 $________
Target disability benefit (60–70% of income):  $________
Employer short-term disability benefit:        −$_______  for ___ months
Employer long-term disability benefit:         −$_______  (typical: 60% capped at ~$10k/month — illustrative — verify current-year value)
Social Security disability (if approved):      −$_______  (unreliable — long approval process)
Gap in disability income:                      $________  → needs individual policy
```

**Key policy features to evaluate:**
- **Own-occupation definition:** Pays if the insured cannot perform their *specific* occupation — the gold standard; more expensive but critical for specialized professionals
- **Any-occupation definition:** Only pays if insured cannot work *any* job for which they are qualified — much weaker protection
- **Benefit period:** How long benefits are paid (2 years, 5 years, to age 65, or lifetime)
- **Elimination period:** How long the insured must be disabled before benefits begin (30, 60, 90, 180 days — longer = lower premium)
- **Cost-of-living adjustment (COLA):** Benefits increase with inflation — important for long benefit periods
- **Future increase option (FIO):** Right to increase coverage as income grows, without new medical underwriting

## 4. Business Insurance Checklist

**When to use:** Business owner's insurance coverage review.

**General Liability (GL):**
- [ ] Covers bodily injury and property damage claims from third parties
- [ ] Typical coverage: $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*
- [ ] Required by most commercial leases and client contracts

**Professional Liability / Errors & Omissions (E&O):**
- [ ] Covers claims arising from professional services, advice, or errors
- [ ] Required for: consultants, advisors, accountants, attorneys, designers, healthcare providers
- [ ] Claims-made policy: must be active when the claim is filed, not just when the incident occurred

**Business Owners Policy (BOP):**
- [ ] Bundles: GL + commercial property + business interruption
- [ ] Best for small businesses with physical location or significant equipment
- [ ] Often excludes: professional liability, workers' comp, commercial auto, cyber

**Key Person Insurance:**
- [ ] Life or disability insurance on a key owner or employee whose loss would significantly harm the business
- [ ] Business is the owner and beneficiary
- [ ] Proceeds fund: hiring replacement, paying off business debt, or buying out the deceased's interest

**Workers' Compensation:**
- [ ] Required in most states for any business with employees (thresholds vary by state)
- [ ] Covers employee injuries on the job regardless of fault

**Cyber Liability:**
- [ ] First-party: covers the business's own losses from a data breach or cyberattack
- [ ] Third-party: covers claims from customers or partners whose data was compromised
- [ ] Increasingly important for businesses storing customer data or operating online

**Commercial Auto:**
- [ ] Required if employees use vehicles for business purposes (personal auto does not cover business use)

## 5. Umbrella Liability Assessment

**When to use:** Determining whether a personal umbrella policy is needed and at what limit.

**Who needs an umbrella policy:**
- Net worth above ~$500k *(illustrative — verify current-year value)* (assets to protect)
- Investment property owners (rental properties increase liability exposure)
- Business owners with personal guarantees
- Dog owners, pool/trampoline owners (elevated liability risk)
- Teen drivers in the household
- High public profile or social media presence
- Directors or officers of non-profit boards

**How umbrella works:**
- Sits above underlying auto and homeowner's (or renter's) liability limits
- Pays claims that exceed underlying policy limits
- Provides coverage for some claims not covered by underlying policies (libel, slander, personal injury)
- Requires underlying policies to maintain minimum liability limits (typically $300k/$100k auto and $300k HO *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*)

**Sizing guidance:**
- Minimum: $1M (basic protection; very affordable — typically $200–400/year *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*)
- Net worth rule: Coverage at least equal to net worth — if net worth is $2M, at least $2M umbrella
- High-exposure individuals: $5M–$10M *(illustrative — verify current-year value)* if significant public profile, multiple properties, or high income

**Cost:**
- $1M umbrella: ~$200–400/year *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*
- Each additional $1M: ~$75–150/year *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*
- One of the highest value-per-dollar insurance products available
