## 1. Entity Selection Matrix

**When to use:** Client is forming a business or evaluating whether to change entity type.

| Factor | Sole Prop | LLC (Single) | LLC (Multi) | S-Corp | C-Corp |
|--------|-----------|--------------|-------------|--------|--------|
| Liability protection | None | Yes (if maintained) | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| SE tax on profits | All profits | All profits | Per operating agreement | Salary only | N/A |
| Pass-through taxation | Yes (Sch C) | Yes (Sch C) | Yes (K-1) | Yes (K-1) | No (double tax) |
| Equity flexibility | N/A | Flexible | Flexible | Restricted (shareholder cap, one class) | Most flexible |
| Formation complexity | None | Low | Low-moderate | Moderate (+ payroll) | High |
| Annual compliance | None | Moderate | Moderate | High (separate return, payroll) | Highest |
| Ideal for | Freelancers, low risk | Single-owner, growing | Multiple owners | Established net profit | VC-backed, equity comp, exit |

**Decision logic:**
1. Is liability protection needed? → Yes: LLC or corp. No: sole prop acceptable for low-risk.
2. Are there multiple owners? → Yes: Multi-member LLC or corp (not sole prop or SMLLC).
3. Is net profit at a level where SE tax savings exceed admin cost? → Yes: evaluate S-Corp election.
4. Is equity compensation or VC investment likely? → Yes: C-Corp (typically Delaware).
5. Is international business or complex capital structure planned? → C-Corp.

When the choice is tax-driven, coordinate with the Tax Strategist (02) on the breakeven analysis.

## 2. Asset Protection Layering

**When to use:** Client wants to understand how to separate personal and business risk.

**Layer 1 — Entity separation:**
- Each business in its own LLC or corporation
- Personal assets never in the operating entity
- Rental properties in separate LLCs (not co-owned with operating business)

**Layer 2 — Operational formality:**
- Maintain separate bank accounts for each entity
- No commingling of personal and business funds
- Document major decisions with resolutions or written consents
- Charge market-rate rent if business operates from personal property

**Layer 3 — Holding company structure (advanced):**
- Operating LLC owned by a holding LLC or trust
- Limits creditor reach: a creditor of the operating LLC cannot easily reach the holding LLC's assets
- Adds complexity and cost — appropriate when operating entity carries significant liability risk

**Layer 4 — Insurance:**
- General liability insurance (first line of defense before entity structure)
- Professional liability / E&O
- Umbrella policy
- D&O insurance (for boards and officers)

**Key risks that defeat protection:**
- Personal guarantees on business debt (the guarantee overrides LLC protection)
- Commingling funds or failing to maintain formalities ("piercing the corporate veil")
- Fraudulent transfers of assets to defeat known creditors

## 3. Operating Agreement Checklist

**When to use:** Client is forming or updating an LLC's operating agreement.

- [ ] Member identification (names, ownership percentages, initial capital contributions)
- [ ] Management structure: member-managed or manager-managed?
- [ ] Voting rights and thresholds (simple majority, supermajority for major decisions)
- [ ] Distribution policy: when and how profits are distributed
- [ ] Capital accounts: how member equity is tracked
- [ ] Transfer restrictions: can members sell or transfer their interest freely?
- [ ] Right of first refusal: must departing members offer interest to remaining members first?
- [ ] Buy-sell trigger events: death, disability, divorce, bankruptcy, voluntary departure
- [ ] Valuation method for buyouts: formula, appraisal, or agreed price?
- [ ] Non-compete and non-solicitation provisions for departing members
- [ ] Dispute resolution: mediation or arbitration before litigation?
- [ ] Governing law: which state's law applies?
- [ ] Admission of new members: consent required? What percentage?

## 4. Contract Risk Review Checklist

**When to use:** Client is reviewing or negotiating a business contract.

**High-priority clauses to locate and evaluate:**
- [ ] **Indemnification:** Who indemnifies whom for what? Is indemnification mutual or one-sided?
- [ ] **Limitation of liability:** Is there a cap on damages? Is it limited to contract value? Are consequential damages excluded?
- [ ] **Intellectual property:** Who owns work product created under the contract? Are licenses granted back?
- [ ] **Non-compete / non-solicitation:** Scope, geography, and duration. Is it enforceable in the applicable state?
- [ ] **Termination:** What triggers termination? Is there a cure period? What are the consequences?
- [ ] **Payment terms:** Net 30/60/90? Late payment penalties? Disputed invoice process?
- [ ] **Governing law and venue:** Which state's law applies? Where must disputes be litigated?
- [ ] **Force majeure:** Does it cover the relevant risks? Is "pandemic" or "supply chain" included?
- [ ] **Confidentiality:** Duration, what's covered, exceptions. Is it mutual?

## 5. Buy-Sell Agreement Guide

**When to use:** Multi-owner business without a buy-sell agreement, or reviewing an existing one.

**Why it matters:** Without a buy-sell, a co-owner's death or departure can force the business to take
on an unwanted partner (heir, creditor, ex-spouse) or trigger a valuation dispute at the worst
possible time.

**Trigger events to address:**
- Death of an owner
- Permanent disability of an owner
- Voluntary departure (resignation)
- Involuntary departure (termination for cause)
- Divorce (preventing an ex-spouse from becoming a co-owner)
- Bankruptcy of an owner
- Retirement

**Structure types:**
- **Cross-purchase:** Each owner buys insurance on the other(s); upon death, buys out the deceased's interest. Works best for 2–3 owners.
- **Entity redemption:** The company buys insurance on each owner; the company redeems the interest on death/departure. Better for larger groups.
- **Hybrid:** Combination — entity has right of first refusal, then surviving owners can exercise.

**Valuation methods:**
- Fixed price (set annually): simple but requires discipline to update
- Formula-based (multiple of EBITDA, revenue): automatic but may not reflect market
- Independent appraisal: most accurate, most expensive, most time-consuming
- Agreed-upon appraisal process: trigger appraisal at the time of event

**Funding:** Life insurance is the most common funding mechanism for the death trigger (coordinate
with the Insurance Specialist (08)). Disability buyout insurance covers the disability trigger.
Without funding, the buyout obligation may require debt financing or installment payments.
