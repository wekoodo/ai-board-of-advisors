## 1. Site Selection & Market Saturation

**When to use:** Deciding whether a market and a specific site support a self-storage facility (new
supply or acquisition).

**Trade area:** Self-storage demand is intensely local — typically a 3–5 mile radius in suburban
markets and 1–3 miles in dense urban ones *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*. Define the
trade area around the site, then analyze demand and supply within it.

**Saturation — net rentable square feet (NRSF) per capita:**
```
Market Saturation = Total competitive NRSF in trade area / Trade-area population
```
A widely used rule of thumb treats roughly **7–10 NRSF per capita** as a balance point: below it the
market may be undersupplied, above it likely oversupplied *(illustrative — verify current-year
value)*. Always adjust for local demand drivers — the per-capita number is a starting screen, not a
verdict.

**Demand drivers (the "5 Ds" and growth):** population density and growth, household mobility (moves
drive demand), housing mix (apartments/renters use more storage), small-business density, and
disruptive life events. Rising population and multifamily development are positive signals.

**Site quality:**
- [ ] Visibility and traffic count (a storage facility is partly a billboard)
- [ ] Access and ease of ingress/egress
- [ ] Competition: count facilities, occupancy, rates, and any announced new supply in the pipeline
- [ ] Zoning and entitlement feasibility
- [ ] Climate-control demand for the local climate and product mix

**Output:** a market verdict (supports new supply / favors acquisition / pass) with the
per-capita saturation figure, competition map, and demand drivers stated.

## 2. Development Pro Forma

**When to use:** Underwriting a ground-up (or major expansion) self-storage development.

**Lease-up curve:** A new facility leases up over time, not on day one. Model a monthly absorption
curve to stabilized occupancy — commonly a 24–36 month lease-up to a stabilized occupancy in the
~85–90% range *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*. The lease-up period carries negative or
thin cash flow and concession risk; model it explicitly.

**Stabilized NOI:**
```
Potential Gross Income (PGI) = Stabilized occupancy × NRSF × market rate/sq ft
Effective Gross Income (EGI) = PGI + ancillary income − vacancy/credit loss − concessions
NOI = EGI − Operating Expenses
```
Include ancillary income (admin fees, tenant insurance/protection, late fees, retail).

**Yield-on-Cost vs. market cap rate (the development spread):**
```
Yield-on-Cost = Stabilized NOI / Total Development Cost (land + hard + soft + carry)
Development Spread = Yield-on-Cost − Market (exit) Cap Rate
```
The spread is the developer's reward for taking development and lease-up risk. Developers commonly
look for a spread on the order of ~150–200+ bps over the prevailing cap rate *(illustrative — verify
current-year value)*; a thin or negative spread means the market price is better than building. Quote
the live cap rate for the market and class.

**Stress test:** flex lease-up speed, stabilized rate, construction cost, and exit cap rate; confirm
the deal survives a slower lease-up and cap-rate expansion.

**Output:** stabilized NOI, total cost, yield-on-cost, development spread vs. market cap rate, and
a sensitivity table.

## 3. Operations & Revenue Management

**When to use:** Optimizing the income and efficiency of an operating facility.

**Existing-Customer Rate Increases (ECRI):** The core revenue lever in self-storage. Because moving
out is costly and inconvenient for tenants, in-place customers tolerate periodic rate increases.
Apply structured, scheduled increases to seated tenants (often beginning a few months after move-in
and repeating on a cadence) *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*, watching the resulting
move-out (churn) rate. The discipline: raise until incremental churn cost outweighs incremental
revenue.

**Occupancy vs. rate optimization:** Maximize **revenue**, not occupancy. The two trade off:
```
Unit Revenue = Occupancy × Achieved Rate
```
Very high physical occupancy (e.g., >90–95%) usually signals underpriced units — raise street rates;
low occupancy signals overpriced units or weak demand — adjust rate and concessions *(illustrative —
verify current-year value)*. Manage by unit type, since demand and pricing power vary by size and
climate control.

**Expense ratio:** Self-storage is a low-operating-expense asset class. The operating expense ratio
(OpEx / EGI) commonly runs in the ~30–40% range *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*; major
line items are property taxes, payroll/management, marketing, insurance, repairs/maintenance, and
utilities. Benchmark each line against comparable facilities to find leakage.

**Ancillary income & technology:** Tenant insurance/protection plans, retail (locks, boxes), admin
fees, and late fees materially lift EGI. Online rentals, dynamic pricing software, and remote/
kiosk management can cut payroll and sharpen revenue management.

**Output:** an operations diagnostic — rate vs. occupancy posture, ECRI cadence, expense-ratio
comparison, and ancillary-income opportunities.

## 4. Acquisition Due-Diligence Checklist

**When to use:** Before forming a view on a self-storage acquisition.

**Financial:**
- [ ] Trailing 12-month actuals (T-12) and rent roll; reconcile to bank statements, not just owner figures
- [ ] In-place NOI vs. pro forma stabilized NOI — is the price based on actual or hoped-for income?
- [ ] Expense ratio vs. benchmark; identify under-reported or owner-suppressed expenses (taxes will reassess)
- [ ] Cap rate implied by the price (NOI / Price) vs. market for the class/market *(illustrative —
      verify current-year value)*

**Operational:**
- [ ] Physical vs. economic occupancy (concessions, delinquencies, non-paying "occupied" units)
- [ ] Street rates vs. in-place rates — is there embedded ECRI upside or is the facility maxed out?
- [ ] Unit mix vs. local demand; climate-control share; ancillary income capture
- [ ] Management quality, staffing, software, and online presence/reviews

**Physical & legal:**
- [ ] Property condition (roofs, doors, paving, security, drainage); deferred maintenance and capex needs
- [ ] Environmental (Phase I), zoning/CO compliance, survey/title, encroachments
- [ ] New supply pipeline in the trade area (permits/announcements) that could pressure rates

**Market:**
- [ ] Trade-area saturation (NRSF per capita) and demand drivers (re: Site Selection framework)
- [ ] Comparable facility occupancy and rates

**Tax & structure (refer to Tax Strategist 02):**
- [ ] Cost-segregation and depreciation opportunity; 1031 exchange eligibility and timeline

**Output:** a go/no-go-to-LOI summary separating in-place reality from upside, with the open
diligence items and capex needs flagged.
