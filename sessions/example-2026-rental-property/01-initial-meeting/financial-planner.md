## The short answer

**Your cash position can support a first rental, but only just — and only if you treat your $15,000 emergency fund as untouchable and refuse to use it for any part of this deal.** On a ~$240,000 property, a 20%-down purchase consumes nearly all of your $47,000 of investable cash and leaves you thin on the *post-closing* reserves a landlord actually needs. That doesn't make this a "no." It makes it a "yes, if you size the purchase to leave a real cushion behind." My recommendation is to either (a) target a lower price point / higher-down structure that leaves you 6+ months of *combined* (personal + property) reserves, or (b) spend the next 6–12 months adding ~$10–15k to cash before you buy. The deal itself — rent comps, vacancy, true operating costs — is the Real Estate Advisor's (06) call; my job is whether the *household balance sheet* survives it.

## The affordability math — and why $47k is tighter than it looks

The trap with a first rental is counting only the down payment. Here is the realistic cash required at a ~$240,000 purchase, financed:

| Cash need | Illustrative amount | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Down payment (20%) | ~$48,000 | Investment-property loans typically require 20–25% down *(illustrative — verify with a lender)* |
| Closing costs | ~$6,000–$12,000 | Roughly 2.5–5% of price; varies by market |
| Initial repairs / make-ready | ~$3,000–$8,000 | Even a "rent-ready" home needs something |
| **Cash to close (subtotal)** | **~$57,000–$68,000** | |
| Post-closing reserves (separate!) | ~$10,000–$15,000 | 6+ months of mortgage + taxes + insurance + a capex buffer |

Compare that to your **$47,000**. At a true 20%-down purchase, you are **already short of the cash-to-close on a $240k home before reserves**, which means a $240k property at 20% down is likely out of reach without either touching the emergency fund (do not) or finding more cash. This is the single most important finding from my chair, and it's why "can we afford it?" deserves a more precise answer than yes/no.

What *does* fit $47,000 cleanly:
- A **lower price point** (e.g., a property in the ~$180–200k range) where 20% down + closing + make-ready lands around $45–55k and still leaves a reserve, **or**
- The same ~$240k home with a **6–12 month savings runway** added first, **or**
- A different financing structure the Real Estate Advisor (06) and a lender can model.

The Real Estate Advisor (06) owns the deal-level numbers — rent vs. the ~$1,900 comps, vacancy, maintenance %, and whether the property cash-flows at all. **My point is upstream of the deal:** whatever property you choose, the purchase must leave your $15k emergency fund untouched *and* leave a separate property reserve behind. If a given deal can't clear both bars, it's the wrong deal for your balance sheet — regardless of how good the property looks.

## How this fits your broader financial picture

Three planning issues deserve weight before you commit $47k to a single illiquid asset:

1. **Your emergency fund is light, and a rental makes it lighter-feeling.** $15k is roughly 3 months of core expenses for a dual-income household. The day you become landlords, your "emergency fund" implicitly has to cover *two* households' surprises — yours and the property's. I'd want you at **4–6 months personal reserves *plus* a dedicated property reserve**, not one pot doing double duty.

2. **A baby is "within a few years."** That is a material cash-flow event — potential leave with reduced income (especially relevant on Priya's larger salary), childcare costs, and a strong desire for liquidity. Locking ~$47k into an illiquid rental right before a planned income dip and expense spike is the timing risk I'd most want you to sit with. It doesn't kill the idea; it argues for *not* draining yourselves to the floor to do it.

3. **Retirement is modest for 35 — make sure the rental isn't crowding it out.** ~$70k combined at 35 suggests you're contributing but not maxing. Before a rental becomes the primary wealth vehicle, confirm you're at least capturing **every dollar of employer match** (free, guaranteed return) and ideally funding a Roth IRA each *(contribution limits are year-specific — illustrative, verify current-year value)*. A rental is a fine wealth-builder, but not at the expense of matched, tax-advantaged retirement space you can never get back.

I'm **not** steering you away from the rental. I'm making the cost visible: this concentrates a large share of your liquid net worth into one illiquid, hands-on, locally-correlated asset right as you plan a family. If you go in with eyes open and proper reserves, that can be a sound wealth move. If you go in stretched, an early vacancy or a $6k repair becomes a household crisis.

## Financial guardrails I'd set before you buy

1. **Emergency fund is sacred.** The $15k does not fund any part of this purchase — not the down payment, not closing, not make-ready. If a deal requires touching it, the deal is too big.
2. **Separate property reserve, separate account.** Hold **6 months of the property's full carrying cost** (mortgage + taxes + insurance + HOA if any) *plus* a capex buffer, distinct from your personal emergency fund, before you take possession.
3. **Cap the cash committed.** I'd keep total cash-out (down + closing + make-ready) to a level that leaves **at least $25k liquid** across your personal emergency fund and property reserve combined. At today's cash, that points you toward a **lower price point than $240k** unless you save more first.
4. **Protect the match first.** Confirm you're capturing 100% of both employer matches before redirecting savings to the property.
5. **Don't stop retirement contributions to fund the rental.** If the rental only works by pausing 401(k)/Roth funding, that's a signal it's too large for now.
6. **Stress-test for 3 simultaneous hits:** 2 months vacancy + a $5k repair + one of you out on leave. If the household can absorb that without touching the emergency fund, you're appropriately reserved. If not, downsize the purchase.

This intersects a few other chairs. The Real Estate Advisor (06) should pressure-test the actual deal economics, financing, and whether it cash-flows. The **Tax Strategist (02)** is genuinely additive here — rental depreciation, deductible expenses, and how a second source of (passive) income interacts with your W-2 picture will change the after-tax return and your withholding. And once you own a rental, the **Insurance Specialist (08)** should weigh in on landlord/dwelling coverage and an umbrella policy, since your liability profile changes the day a tenant moves in.

## Bottom line

Can you afford a first rental? **At a right-sized price point with disciplined reserves, yes — at $240k with 20% down today, not without overextending.** Is it sound right now? **It can be**, provided you keep the emergency fund intact, build a separate property reserve, protect retirement contributions, and account for the baby-planning timeline. The cleanest path is to either target a lower entry price or spend the next 6–12 months adding to cash — both of which fit comfortably inside your stated decision horizon.
