# Real Estate Advisor — Initial Meeting

Priya and Marcus — welcome. Here is my honest read up front: **the deal you've described, as priced, does not clear the bar of a sound first rental.** A ~$240,000 home renting for ~$1,900/month fails the basic screens real estate investors use, and on standard financing it most likely produces **negative monthly cash flow** before you've collected a dollar of profit. That doesn't mean rentals are wrong for you — it means *this price-to-rent ratio in this market* is a tough entry point, and you'll need to be selective. Below I show you exactly how to evaluate any deal so you can tell a good one from a bad one yourself.

I'm focusing on the property economics, location/due-diligence, and landlord realities. Whether your *overall finances* can support this — emergency fund, debt, retirement trajectory — is the Financial Planner's (01) call, and I'll cross-refer rather than duplicate it.

## 1. The quick screens — does this deal even deserve a deep look?

Two back-of-envelope tests every investor runs *before* spending hours on a property:

| Screen | Rule of thumb | Your example deal | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1% rule** | Monthly rent ≥ 1% of purchase price | $1,900 ÷ $240,000 = **0.79%** | **Fails** (want ≥ $2,400/mo at this price) |
| **Gross rent multiplier (GRM)** | Lower is better; ~7–10 is typical for SFR | $240,000 ÷ ($1,900 × 12) = **10.5** | High / weak |

These are *screens*, not verdicts — they're how you decide which listings to underwrite and which to skip. A property that misses the 1% rule by this much rarely cash-flows on leverage. You'd need either a **lower purchase price (~$190k)** or **higher rent (~$2,400)** to hit 1%.

## 3. Return metrics — what to compute on every deal

Use these to compare *this* property against *another* property (or against simply investing the cash — a question for the Investment Advisor, 05).

| Metric | Formula | This deal |
|---|---|---|
| **Cap rate** | Annual NOI ÷ purchase price | (~$16,800 ÷ $240,000) ≈ **6.0–6.5%** — financing-independent, good for comparing properties |
| **Cash-on-cash return** | Annual pre-tax cash flow ÷ total cash invested | **Negative** here (cash flow is negative) — this is the number that tells you if leverage is working |
| **DSCR** | NOI ÷ annual debt service | ~$16,800 ÷ ~$14,700 ≈ **1.1** — thin; lenders often want ≥ 1.20–1.25 |

A negative cash-on-cash means you'd be **feeding the property monthly** and betting entirely on appreciation and slow loan paydown. That can work — but it's speculation, not income investing, and it's a heavy bet for first-timers with a thin cash cushion.

## 4. What would make a deal actually work

You're not stuck with the example. Target properties where the math flips:

1. **Buy below the median** — a $190k–$210k home renting for $1,800–$1,900 dramatically improves the ratio. Look for cosmetically dated (not structurally compromised) homes.
2. **Higher-rent submarkets** — proximity to hospitals (Priya's network), good school zones, and employment centers supports rent and tenant quality.
3. **Larger down payment** to cut debt service — but this collides directly with your "don't drain the safety net" rule. The Financial Planner (01) should weigh this trade-off.
4. **Force appreciation / value-add** — light rehab that lifts rent, or a legal accessory unit, changes the equation. Higher complexity for a first-timer, though.
5. **Negotiate price** — at a 0.79% rent ratio, you need price discipline. Make offers that pencil; walk from ones that don't.

## 5. Location & due-diligence checklist (condensed)

Before you ever go under contract:

- **Neighborhood:** rent comps (not list prices — actual signed leases), vacancy trends, days-on-market for rentals, crime, school ratings, employer stability, pride-of-ownership signals.
- **The property:** professional inspection, roof/HVAC/foundation/plumbing/electrical age, permit history on any additions, flood zone status (affects insurance — 08).
- **The numbers:** verify *actual* property tax (reassessment on sale can spike it), get a real insurance quote (08), confirm rent with a local property manager, not your hopes.
- **Regulatory:** local landlord-tenant law, eviction timelines/difficulty, rent-control or licensing/registration rules, short-term-rental restrictions if relevant.

## 6. Landlord realities for first-timers

- **You're starting a small business**, not buying a bond. Tenant screening, maintenance calls at 11pm, turnovers, the occasional non-paying tenant and a months-long eviction — these are the job.
- **Self-managing saves ~8–10%** of rent but costs time and emotional bandwidth. Budget for management even if you self-manage at first; you may not want to forever.
- **Liquidity is poor.** Selling takes months and ~6–9% in transaction costs. This is a 5–10+ year hold to make the math and tax benefits work — not money you can recall quickly.
- **Concentration risk:** one property in one local market = undiversified. A single bad tenant or a roof can erase a year of returns.
- **Tax upside is real** (depreciation can shelter income) and worth modeling — that's the **Tax Strategist's (02)** domain; loop them in before you assume the after-tax picture rescues the pre-tax cash flow. Note depreciation helps with *taxable income*, not your *monthly cash* shortfall.
