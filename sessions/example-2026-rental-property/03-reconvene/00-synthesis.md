## The bottom line

**The board's unanimous recommendation: do not buy the $240,000 property — but yes, become landlords, on a tighter set of terms, within your own 6–12 month horizon.** Both advisors who studied this — the Financial Planner and the Real Estate Advisor — reached the same verdict by two completely independent routes, and that agreement is the strongest signal in this whole session.

- The **Real Estate Advisor** looked at the *deal itself* and found it doesn't pencil: it fails the 1% screen (0.79%) and runs **negative cash flow every month** — you'd be feeding the property, not earning from it.
- The **Financial Planner** looked at *your balance sheet* and found it doesn't fit: cash-to-close (~$66k–$69k) exceeds your ~$47,000 investable cash *before a single dollar of reserve*, and it fails all four affordability tests.

When the deal analysis and the household analysis fail at the same time, the lesson isn't "find clever financing." It's **"this is the wrong target."** The fix is a different, smaller, better-screened property — bought after you've added some cash. Here is exactly how the board sees it, and what to do.

## Where the two advisors agree (the consensus)

The board's confidence rests on this: on every point that matters, the two advisors independently agree.

1. **The $240k property is a pass today** — the Real Estate Advisor on economics, the Financial Planner on affordability. Same answer, two routes.
2. **Your $15,000 emergency fund is sacred.** It funds *no part* of any purchase — not down payment, not closing, not make-ready. Both advisors exclude it from every "available cash" figure by design.
3. **Reserves beyond cash-to-close are mandatory, not optional.** You need a *separate* property reserve (~$15,000 *(illustrative)*) sitting in its own account **before you take possession** — distinct from the emergency fund. Two pots, two jobs.
4. **The right target is a lower price point: ~$185k–$210k**, with rent *held* near ~$1,800–$1,900. This is the overlap of "where the deal can pencil" (Real Estate) and "what your cash can absorb" (Financial Planner).
5. **The binding constraint is cash/reserves — not the down payment.** Even at $170k–$185k, today's $47k barely funds the reserve. That's why saving more, not financing tricks, is the lever.
6. **Both worksheets are reusable and run in order on every candidate:** the Real Estate Advisor's deal-screen first (*does it pencil?*), then the Financial Planner's four-part affordability scorecard (*can your household absorb it?*). Proceed **only if it passes both.**
7. **Time the purchase around — never into — a parental-leave year.** With a baby planned and the larger income on Priya's side, an income dip is a *planned event* to reserve for on purpose, not a tail risk.

## The one remaining tension — and how the board resolves it

There is exactly one place the advisors lean differently, and notably, **each conceded the point to the other.** The board adopts the resolution they converged on.

| The tension | Real Estate Advisor's instinct | Financial Planner's discipline |
|---|---|---|
| **Appreciation thesis vs. cash-flow discipline** | A quality, appreciating location (good schools, hospitals near Priya's network) can justify a *thin* deal — loan paydown + appreciation build wealth even at near-zero cash flow. | For a *first-time, single-property, undiversified* landlord planning a family, **cash flow and reserves come first.** Appreciation is a bonus you don't underwrite on. |

**Board resolution — discipline wins, and here's the elegant reason the Real Estate Advisor gave for it:** an appreciation bet only pays *if you can hold through the bad year* — the vacancy, the roof, the parental leave. **Reserves are precisely what keep the appreciation bet alive.** So the two views are not really opposed:

> **Use the appreciation thesis to choose *which* neighborhood. Use cash flow and reserves to decide whether you're *allowed to buy at all*.** Buy in a location with a credible appreciation story — but only at a price and reserve structure that passes all four affordability tests.

A second, related trade-off the board flags and resolves: the deal-screen worksheet lists "**larger down payment**" as a way to force a deal positive. Arithmetically true — but for *your* situation it backfires, because piling more of your limited cash into the $240k property is exactly what fails the affordability and liquidity tests. **Resolution: solve negative cash flow by buying a cheaper, better-screened property — not by shoveling more cash into an over-priced one.** A bigger down payment that empties your reserves converts a cash-flow problem into a solvency problem.

## Your integrated action plan

A single, sequenced path that satisfies *both* gates. The first three moves you can start this week.

1. **Keep the $15,000 emergency fund untouched — permanently off the table.** It funds no part of any purchase. If a deal only works by dipping into it, the deal is too big. Full stop.
2. **Set a savings target of ~$70,000 total cash before any offer** — roughly **$15k–$20k more** than today, achievable inside your 6–12 month horizon on a $145k income. This single move fixes the cash-to-close shortfall *and* funds the ≥$25k liquid floor. It is the highest-leverage thing you can do. *(All figures illustrative — verify current-year value.)*
3. **Open a separate property-reserve account** and build it toward ~$15,000 *(illustrative)* — visibly distinct from your emergency fund. Don't commingle. It must be funded *before* you take possession.
4. **Re-anchor your price ceiling to ~$185k–$210k**, not $240k. Hunt cosmetically-dated-but-structurally-sound homes in **higher-rent, good-school, stable-employer submarkets** (hospitals near Priya's network, schools, government).
5. **Pull *signed-lease* rent comps — not asking rents** — to confirm rent actually holds near ~$1,900 at a ~$190k price in your target areas. If it doesn't, the window doesn't exist in this market; wait or look elsewhere. (This is the single number that drives all the underwriting.)
6. **On any specific candidate, get the two numbers that decide everything first:** the **actual post-sale (reassessed) property tax** and a **real DP-3 landlord-insurance quote**. Until these are real, every cash-flow figure is a guess.
7. **Run both worksheets on the same deal, in order:** the deal-screen worksheet (does it pencil?), then the affordability scorecard (can you absorb it?). **Make an offer only if it passes both** with the emergency fund untouched.
8. **Capture 100% of both employer 401(k) matches — and never pause retirement contributions to feed the rental.** A property that needs your retirement dollars to survive is, by definition, too big.
9. **Once you're under contract on a right-sized deal, loop in two more advisors:**
   - **Tax Strategist (02)** — depreciation and how passive rental losses interact with your W-2 income. Note: this shelters *taxable income*, not your *monthly cash*, so it softens but does not rescue a negative deal.
   - **Insurance Specialist (08)** — the DP-3 landlord policy and umbrella liability, in place the day a tenant moves in.
