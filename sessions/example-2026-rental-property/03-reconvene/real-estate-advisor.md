## 3. The integrated target — what actually works for you

Here is the synthesis neither worksheet states on its own. **A deal must clear BOTH gates at once**, and the two gates pull in *different directions* — which is the whole point:

- **My deal gate (economics):** to cash-flow on leverage, you want a **lower price-to-rent ratio** — ideally a property nearer the 1% rule. In your market that means a **~$185k–$210k home that still rents at ~$1,800–$1,900**, or a higher-rent submarket.
- **The Planner's gate (balance sheet):** to pass the ≥$25k-liquid floor and the 3-hit stress test, your **cash-to-close must leave ~$25k liquid after closing** — which at today's ~$62k cash means a price around **~$185–200k AND/OR ~$10–15k more saved**.

**These two gates overlap in a narrow but real window:**

| Target characteristic | Set by | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Price ~$185k–$210k** | Both gates | Improves rent ratio (mine) *and* fits cash/reserves (Planner's) |
| **Rent ~$1,800–$1,900 held** | My gate | Below ~$185k the ratio finally approaches 1%; don't let rent fall with price |
| **~$60k–$75k total cash before offer** | Planner's gate | Funds cash-to-close *and* leaves the ≥$25k liquid floor |
| **Verified post-sale tax + real DP-3 quote** | Both gates | These swing my cash flow *and* the Planner's monthly carry (B1–B5) |

The honest read: **at $185–200k with rent holding near $1,900, the deal moves from clearly-negative toward roughly breakeven** *(self-managed, pending verified tax/insurance)* — not a cash-flow home run, but no longer a property you feed every month. Combined with the Planner's "raise cash to ~$70k" path, that is a **genuinely financeable, survivable first rental within your 6–12 month horizon.** That's a real "yes, here's how," not a soft "no."

## 4. The remaining tension — appreciation thesis vs. strict cash-flow discipline

This is the one place we lean differently, and you should hear it openly.

- **My instinct as a real estate person** is that a stable, appreciating location with good schools and employers (hospitals near Priya's network) can justify a *thin* deal, because loan paydown + appreciation build wealth even when monthly cash flow is near zero. There's a real case for buying quality and being patient.
- **The Planner's discipline** is that for *your specific household* — first rental, no landlord experience, a planned parental leave with the larger income on Priya's side, a thin cushion — **cash flow and reserves must come first**, and appreciation is a bonus you don't underwrite on.

**For first-timers in your position, the Planner's discipline wins, and I defer to it.** Here's why I, the real estate advisor, am the one saying that: an appreciation bet only pays if you can *hold through* the bad year — the vacancy, the roof, the leave. If a thin-cash-flow property forces you to sell into a down market or raid the emergency fund, the appreciation thesis never gets to play out. **Reserves are what let you keep the appreciation bet alive.** So the tension resolves cleanly: **buy in a location with a credible appreciation thesis (my contribution), but only at a price/reserve structure that passes all four of the Planner's tests (their contribution).** Appreciation is the reason to choose *which* neighborhood; cash flow and reserves are the reason you're *allowed to buy at all*.

## 5. What to do over the next 6–12 months

A concrete, sequenced plan that satisfies both gates:

1. **Set the savings target now.** Aim for **~$70k total cash** before making any offer. That's roughly **~$10k–$15k more** than today — achievable inside your horizon on a $145k income, and it's the single highest-leverage move you can make. It simultaneously fixes the cash-to-close shortfall *and* funds the ≥$25k liquid floor.
2. **Stand up a separate property-reserve account** (Planner's Part B, ~$15k target) so it's visibly distinct from your $15k emergency fund. Don't commingle.
3. **Re-anchor your price ceiling to ~$185k–$210k**, not $240k. Hunt cosmetically-dated-not-structurally-compromised homes in higher-rent, stable-employer, good-school submarkets.
4. **Pull *signed-lease* rent comps** (Stage 1 of my checklist), not asking rents — this confirms whether ~$1,900 rent holds at a ~$190k price in your target areas. If it doesn't, the window doesn't exist in this market and you wait or look elsewhere.
5. **On any specific candidate, get the two numbers that decide everything:** the **actual post-sale property tax** and a **real DP-3 landlord-insurance quote** (tag Insurance Specialist, 08). These feed *both* my underwriting worksheet (B2) *and* the Planner's carry (B1–B5). Until they're real, every cash-flow figure is a guess.
6. **Run both worksheets on the same deal, in order:** my deal-screen + cash-flow worksheet first (does it pencil?), then the Planner's affordability scorecard (can your household absorb it?). **Proceed only if it passes both.** A deal that pencils but fails the scorecard is still a no; a deal that fits your cash but bleeds money monthly is also a no.
7. **Plan the purchase around the parental-leave timeline.** The Planner is right that this is a *planned event*, not a tail risk. If a baby is likely within the window, either buy and fully reserve *before* the leave, or wait until after income normalizes. Don't close a first rental in the same year as a leave.
8. **Loop in the Tax Strategist (02)** before assuming depreciation rescues the picture — it shelters *taxable income*, not your *monthly cash*, so it doesn't change the affordability math, only the after-tax return.
