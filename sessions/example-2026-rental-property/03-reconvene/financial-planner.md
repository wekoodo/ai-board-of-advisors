# Financial Planner — Reconvene

Priya and Marcus — I've now reviewed the Real Estate Advisor's (06) two artifacts alongside my own affordability & reserves worksheet. The deal economics are in, and they change my answer from "right-size it" to something more pointed. Here is my updated, integrated view.

## The short answer (revised)

**At $240,000, this is a no — not because your household is weak, but because the deal itself is weak *and* your cash can't fund it.** In Round 1 I said a first rental was reasonable "if you size the purchase to leave a real cushion." The worked numbers now show the $240k property fails on **both** fronts at once: it doesn't pencil as an investment (negative monthly cash flow, fails the 1% screen) **and** it doesn't fit your balance sheet (cash-to-close exceeds your investable cash before a single dollar of reserve). When a deal fails affordability *and* fails its own economics, you don't have a financing problem to solve — you have the wrong target. The path forward is unchanged in direction but firmer in conviction: **lower the price point, save more, protect the emergency fund, and stand up a separate property reserve** before you buy anything.

## What the deal economics changed for me

In Round 1 I deliberately deferred the deal-level numbers to the Real Estate Advisor and confined myself to the household balance sheet. Now that I've seen their underwriting, two findings reinforce my caution and one sharpens it:

| Dimension | My Round 1 view | After seeing 06's underwriting |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash to close** | ~$57k–$68k vs. ~$47k available — short ~$15k | **Confirmed and slightly worse.** 06 models 25% down → cash-to-close ~$66k–$69k. Short ~$19k–$22k. |
| **Does it cash-flow?** | Deferred to 06; flagged "if it doesn't clear both bars, wrong deal" | **Answered: no.** ~−$60 to −$260/mo self-managed, *negative*, and worse if managed. Fails the 1% rule at 0.79%. |
| **Why it matters** | A thin deal stresses a thin balance sheet | **Now compounding.** A *negative-cash-flow* property means the asset itself drains cash monthly — on top of the reserve drain. |

That last row is the real shift. In Round 1 my worry was a one-time overextension at closing plus episodic shocks (vacancy, repair, leave). The underwriting reveals a property that, as priced, would ask you to **feed it every single month** — roughly $700–$3,100/year out of pocket before taxes, in good months. That isn't an emergency-fund problem; it's a structural drag that competes directly with the retirement contributions and baby-fund savings I want you protecting. A negative-cash-flow rental is a bet on appreciation alone, financed by your monthly budget. For a first-time, single-property, undiversified landlord planning a family, that's the opposite of the margin of safety I'd want.

## The path forward — concrete

Nothing here closes the door on becoming landlords. It redirects you to a deal that works on both worksheets at once. Three moves, in order:

1. **Reset the target price to where the deal can pencil *and* fit your cash.** 06 points to the **~$190k–$210k** range (a home renting at ~$1,800–$1,900 dramatically improves the rent-to-price ratio). My worksheet independently lands near the same zone for affordability. The overlap — a **sub-median home around $185k–$205k with verified signed-lease rent comps** — is where both of us say "now run the full numbers." Don't underwrite anything that fails 06's Part A 1% screen first; don't make an offer on anything that fails my four-part Scorecard.
2. **Save the gap over the next 6–12 months — it fits your stated horizon.** Adding **~$15k–$20k** to cash (toward ~$62k–$67k investable + the untouched $15k emergency fund) is what turns even a right-priced deal from "borderline" to "passes with margin." This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do, and you have the runway to do it before anything is under contract.
3. **Protect the emergency fund, then build a *separate* property reserve.** The $15k emergency fund funds **no part** of any purchase — not down payment, not closing, not make-ready. On top of it, stand up a **dedicated property-reserve account holding ~6 months of the property's full carry plus a capex buffer (~$15k on a sub-$210k deal)** before you take possession. Two pots, two jobs: one protects your household, one protects the asset. Never let one do double duty.

Two guardrails carry over unchanged and matter even more now that we know the deal runs negative:

- **Don't pause retirement contributions to feed a rental.** A property that needs your 401(k)/Roth/match dollars to survive is, by definition, too big. Capture 100% of both employer matches first.
- **Respect the baby timeline.** A planned income dip (especially on Priya's larger salary) plus a property that *already* costs you monthly is a collision worth avoiding by carrying more reserve, not less.

## Where 06 and I agree — and the one remaining tension

**We agree completely on the verdict and the direction:**
- The $240k property fails as priced — 06 on the deal economics, me on the balance sheet. Same answer, two independent routes.
- The fix is a **lower price point (~$190k–$210k)** with verified local numbers.
- Reserves beyond cash-to-close are mandatory; 06 explicitly hands reserve-sizing to me, and my Part B sizes it (~$15k).
- Both worksheets are reusable: run 06's Part A screen and full underwriting on every candidate, then my four-part Scorecard, before any offer.

**The one remaining tension is the down-payment lever.** 06's Part D lists "larger down payment" as a way to cut debt service and flip a deal positive — and arithmetically that's true. From the planning chair, I want to flag the trade-off explicitly: putting *more* cash down on a $240k property to force positive cash flow is exactly the move that **fails my Parts A and C** by draining your liquidity to the floor. 06 already notes this collides with "don't drain the safety net" and hands the call to me, so it's a flagged trade-off rather than a real disagreement — but for you the resolution is unambiguous: **solve the cash-flow problem by buying a cheaper, better-screened property, not by shoveling more of your limited cash into an over-priced one.** A bigger down payment that empties your reserves converts a cash-flow problem into a solvency problem. Don't make that trade.

## Two open items that still gate a final "go"

Both worksheets are built on illustrative inputs. Before you trust either:
1. **Verified local numbers** — *signed-lease* rent comps, the *post-sale* (reassessed) property-tax figure, a real DP-3 landlord-insurance quote, and a current investment-property rate and down-payment requirement from an actual lender. These swing the monthly result from "slightly negative" to "clearly negative" or "borderline positive." (06's checklist Stage 3 and my worksheet Parts B–D both depend on these.)
2. **Your true monthly core expenses and actual employer parental-leave terms** — to confirm the $15k really is a sound emergency fund and to size the leave leg of the stress test precisely.

And two referrals remain genuinely additive once you're under contract on a *right-sized* deal: the **Tax Strategist (02)** on depreciation and how passive rental losses interact with your W-2 income (this can soften — though not erase — a modestly negative pre-tax result), and the **Insurance Specialist (08)** on the DP-3 policy and umbrella liability the day a tenant moves in.

## Bottom line

I'm revising Round 1 from "yes, if right-sized" to a firmer **"not this deal, and not at this price — but yes to a smaller, better-screened deal once you've added cash."** The $240k property fails on economics *and* affordability simultaneously, which is actually clarifying: it tells you the answer isn't clever financing, it's a different target. Spend the next 6–12 months saving ~$15k–$20k, hunt in the ~$190k–$210k band using both worksheets, keep the $15k emergency fund sacred, and build a separate property reserve before possession. Do that, and a first rental moves from "overextension" to "sound move." It's the same destination I described in Round 1 — the worked numbers just removed any ambiguity about the route.
