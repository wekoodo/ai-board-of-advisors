# Tax Strategist — Initial Meeting

Maya, the tax outcome of this sale will hinge on **deal structure** more than on the headline price.
Here is how I'd frame it.

## 1. Asset sale vs. stock sale
- **Buyers usually prefer an asset sale** (they get a stepped-up basis and pick which liabilities to
  assume). **Sellers usually prefer a stock/equity sale** (generally cleaner capital-gains treatment).
- As an S-Corp, an asset sale passes gain through to you personally. The character matters: gain on
  goodwill and most intangibles is typically **long-term capital gain**, while gain on depreciated
  equipment can be **ordinary income (depreciation recapture)**.

## 2. Federal rate exposure (illustrative)
- Long-term capital gains are taxed at roughly **0% / 15% / 20%** depending on taxable income
  *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*.
- A **3.8% net investment income tax** may apply above certain MAGI thresholds *(illustrative — verify
  current-year value)*.
- Depreciation recapture on equipment is taxed at ordinary rates (up to the top bracket)
  *(illustrative — verify current-year value)*.

## 3. Levers to reduce tax drag
- **Purchase-price allocation:** negotiate the allocation across asset classes (goodwill vs. equipment
  vs. a covenant not to compete). Allocation shifts how much gain is capital vs. ordinary — coordinate
  with the Corporate Attorney (03), who drafts it into the agreement.
- **Installment sale:** spreading payments over years can defer gain and may keep you out of the top
  bracket in any single year — weigh against buyer credit risk.
- **State tax:** confirm your state's treatment of the gain and whether residency planning is even relevant.

## 4. Coordinate
- Financial Planner (01) should model the **after-tax** proceeds against your retirement goal.
- Corporate Attorney (03) controls the structure and allocation language that drives the tax result.
