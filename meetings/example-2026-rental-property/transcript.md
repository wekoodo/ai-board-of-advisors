## Initial takes

### Real Estate Advisor (06) — convened

**Chair → Real Estate Advisor:** Give Priya and Marcus your honest read on the example deal — $240k purchase, $1,900 rent — using the screens you'd run on any first rental.

**Real Estate Advisor:** I'll give you the verdict up front, then show you the screens. **The deal as priced is a pass.** Two back-of-envelope tests every rental investor runs:

- **1% rule** (monthly rent ÷ price): $1,900 ÷ $240,000 = **0.79%**. You want ≥ 1.0%, or roughly $2,400/month rent at this price. It fails.
- **Gross Rent Multiplier:** $240,000 ÷ ($1,900 × 12) = **10.5**. Typical single-family rentals run 7–10. High.

When I underwrite the full cash flow — financing it at 25% down at an illustrative ~7.25% rate, with mandatory reserves for vacancy (5%), maintenance (10%), and CapEx (5%), plus property taxes and a landlord insurance estimate — the property runs **roughly −$60 to −$260/month** self-managed, worse if you hire a manager. That means you'd be **feeding the property monthly** and betting entirely on appreciation. That's not income investing — it's speculation, and it's a heavy first bet.

Separately, the **cash to close** at 25% down comes in around **$66,000–$69,000** (down + closing). That already exceeds your $47k of investable cash before you've set aside a single dollar of reserve. So even if the economics were better, the cash position doesn't fund this purchase.

The math flips on a different target — something in the **~$185k–$210k** range where rent still holds near ~$1,800–$1,900. That's where the rent ratio approaches 1% and the deal moves toward breakeven. But I'm going to stop here and let the Planner speak to your balance sheet — affordability is their call, not mine.

### Financial Planner (01) — convened

**Chair → Financial Planner:** Same question from your chair — does the household balance sheet support a first rental at the example deal, and if not, what does?

**Financial Planner:** I'll be direct. **At $240k with 20% down, your cash doesn't fit the purchase — and the deal that the Real Estate Advisor just described would feed the property out of your monthly budget, which compounds it.** Here's how I see your balance sheet:

The headline strengths are real: **no credit-card debt**, **stable dual W-2 income**, full employer 401(k) contributions. Those are exactly the conditions under which a first rental is a reasonable thing to *consider*.

The cautions are also real: your **$15k emergency fund is light** — roughly 3 months of core expenses for a dual-income household, where I'd normally want 4–6. Your **retirement balance (~$70k combined at 35) is modest**, so any cash you commit to a rental can't crowd out the matched, tax-advantaged retirement space you can never get back. And — this matters — a **baby is planned within a few years**. That's a material cash-flow event: potential leave with reduced income (especially on Priya's larger salary), childcare costs, and a strong desire for liquidity. Locking $47k into an illiquid asset right before a planned income dip is the timing risk I'd most want you to sit with.

On the cash math: even at 20% down, the realistic cash-to-close on a $240k property — down payment, closing costs, initial repairs — lands around **$57k–$68k**. You have $47k available with the emergency fund excluded, which is exactly the right way to count it. So we're short *before* I've even talked about post-closing reserves. A landlord needs a **separate property reserve** — roughly 6 months of the property's full carrying cost plus a capex buffer — held in its own account, distinct from your personal emergency fund. Two pots, two jobs.

What's the path forward? Two ways the deal becomes a "yes": **(a)** target a lower price point — somewhere in the ~$185k–$200k range where the down payment + closing + reserve actually fits — or **(b)** spend the next 6–12 months adding ~$10k–$15k to cash. Both fit comfortably inside your stated decision horizon.

The headline rule, if you take nothing else from this: **the $15k emergency fund is sacred. It funds no part of any purchase — not the down payment, not closing, not make-ready.** If a deal only works by dipping into it, the deal is too big.

### Chair — quick synthesis of the initial takes

**Board Chair:** Notice what just happened: the Real Estate Advisor analyzed the *property* and concluded the deal doesn't pencil. The Financial Planner analyzed your *balance sheet* and concluded the cash doesn't fit. **Two independent routes, same verdict — and they converge on the same fix:** a lower price point (~$185k–$210k) and/or more cash before any offer. That convergence is the strongest signal in this meeting.

You don't need to memorize their reasoning. You need **reusable tools** you can run on the next property and the one after that. Let me commission those now.

### The Real Estate Advisor reacts

**Board Chair → Real Estate Advisor:** Read the Financial Planner's revised worksheet, especially the new passing-example sub-section in Part C. Does the $200k anchor hold from your side?

**Real Estate Advisor:** It holds, with one explicit condition that I want named so Priya and Marcus don't take the $200k as a generic target. **The $200k passing example assumes rent stays near ~$1,800–$1,900** at that price point. If rent slides with price — say, a $200k home in a weaker submarket only rents for $1,500 — the deal screen on *my* worksheet flips back to negative cash flow, and the affordability passes on the Planner's worksheet become irrelevant because the asset itself drains your reserves monthly.

So the integrated rule is: **the affordability scorecard tells you whether your household can absorb the purchase; my deal screen tells you whether the property generates rather than consumes cash.** Both have to pass on the same property. Priya and Marcus, you'll find the lever that controls this — the single number that drives everything — is **signed-lease rent comps** at your target price point. Not asking rents, not the seller's pro-forma, not the listing's optimistic figure. Stage 1 of the due-diligence checklist walks through how to pull them.

The one trade-off I want to flag — and concede, in this household's case — is that my worksheet lists "**larger down payment**" as a way to force a thin deal positive. Arithmetically that's true. But for *your* situation, putting more cash into a $240k property is exactly the move that **fails the Planner's affordability and liquidity tests**. The right move is to **buy a cheaper, better-screened property — not shovel more cash into an over-priced one.** That's a flagged trade-off rather than a real disagreement; the Planner owns the affordability call and I defer to it.

## Board synthesis

**Board Chair:** Pulling this together for both of you:

1. **The $240k deal is a pass.** Independently confirmed by both advisors — the property doesn't pencil and the cash doesn't fit. When the deal analysis and the balance-sheet analysis fail at the same time, the answer is **not** clever financing. It's a different target.

2. **Your real target is a property in the ~$185k–$210k range, with rent verified at ~$1,800–$1,900 by *signed-lease* comps**, purchased after you've raised total cash to around **~$70k** (roughly $15k–$20k more than today). That's the overlap of "the deal pencils" (Real Estate) and "your household absorbs it" (Financial Planner).

3. **The $15,000 emergency fund is sacred — permanently off the table.** Both advisors exclude it from every "available cash" figure. You add a **separate property reserve** (~$15k illustrative) to its own account before you take possession. Two pots, two jobs.

4. **You have three reusable tools.** Run them in this order on any candidate property:
   - **Part A of the deal-screen worksheet** — the five-minute filter. Most listings die here.
   - **The due-diligence and location checklist** — qualifies the market before you tour, then the property during the contingency window.
   - **Parts B–D of the deal screen + all four parts of the affordability scorecard** — the real underwriting and the household-fit test. **Make an offer only if all of it passes.**

5. **The one tension between the advisors — appreciation thesis vs. strict cash-flow discipline — resolves in favor of discipline for your situation, with an elegant reason the Real Estate Advisor gave for it:** an appreciation bet only pays *if you can hold through the bad year* — the vacancy, the roof, the parental leave. **Reserves are precisely what keep the appreciation bet alive.** So you buy in a neighborhood with a credible appreciation story (good schools, hospitals near Priya's network, stable employers) — but only at a price and reserve structure that clears all four affordability tests.

6. **Time the purchase around — not into — a parental-leave year.** With Priya's larger salary, an income dip is a *planned event* to reserve for on purpose. Don't close a first rental in the same year as a leave.

7. **Two specialists become genuinely additive *once you're under contract on a right-sized deal* — not before.** The **Tax Strategist (02)** on depreciation and how passive rental losses interact with your W-2 income (note: depreciation shelters *taxable income*, not your *monthly cash*, so it softens but never rescues a negative-cash-flow deal). The **Insurance Specialist (08)** on the DP-3 landlord policy and umbrella liability the day a tenant moves in.
