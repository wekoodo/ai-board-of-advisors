## PART A — Quick Screens (the 5-minute filter)

Run these first. They tell you which listings to underwrite and which to skip. They are
*filters*, not verdicts.

| Screen | Formula | Target / rule of thumb | Your number | Pass? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1% rule** | Monthly rent ÷ purchase price | ≥ **1.0%** (≥ 0.8% marginal) | _____ % | ☐ |
| **Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM)** | Purchase price ÷ annual gross rent | **7–10** typical SFR; lower is better | _____ | ☐ |
| **Price vs. local median** | Purchase price ÷ area median | At or below median = easier to cash-flow | _____ | ☐ |
| **Rent vs. comps** | Asking rent vs. *signed* lease comps | Within ±5% of real comps | _____ | ☐ |

**Decision gate:** If the 1% rule comes in below ~0.85% **and** you have no explicit value-add
or appreciation thesis, stop here. Do not spend hours underwriting a deal the screen already
told you will bleed cash.

## PART B — Full Cash-Flow Underwriting (the real test)

### B1. Acquisition & Financing

| Item | Input | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Purchase price | $ _______ | |
| Down payment % | ____ % | Investment SFR typically **20–25% down** *(illustrative — verify with lenders)* |
| Down payment $ | $ _______ | price × down % |
| Loan amount | $ _______ | price − down payment |
| Interest rate | ____ % *(illustrative — verify current-year value)* | Investment rates run ~0.5–0.75% above owner-occupied |
| Loan term | ____ yrs | 30-yr fixed assumed |
| Closing costs (2–4%) | $ _______ | on top of down payment |
| **Cash to close** | **$ _______** | **down payment + closing costs** |

### B2. Monthly Operating Cash Flow

| Line | Monthly $ | Default basis |
|---|---|---|
| Gross scheduled rent | + $ _______ | use *signed-lease* comps, not asking rents |
| Vacancy reserve | − $ _______ | **5%** of rent (≈ one turnover/yr) |
| Maintenance / repairs | − $ _______ | **10%** of rent; older homes higher |
| CapEx reserve (roof, HVAC, etc.) | − $ _______ | **5%** of rent — real but lumpy |
| Property management | − $ _______ | **0%** self-managed / **8–10%** if hired |
| **Operating expenses subtotal** | **− $ _______** | sum of the four reserves above |
| **Net Operating Income (NOI), monthly** | **= $ _______** | gross rent − operating expenses |
| Mortgage P&I | − $ _______ *(illustrative — verify)* | from B1 |
| Property taxes | − $ _______ | **verify actual rate — reassesses on sale** |
| Insurance (landlord / DP-3) | − $ _______ | get a real quote — tag Insurance Specialist (08) |
| **Monthly cash flow** | **= $ _______** | NOI − P&I − taxes − insurance |

> **Reserves are mandatory, not padding.** Vacancy, maintenance, and CapEx are the costs
> first-time landlords forget and then get blindsided by. A water heater, a tenant turnover, a
> roof — they average out to real monthly money even in years you don't write the check.

### B3. Annualize for the Return Metrics

| Item | Formula | Your number |
|---|---|---|
| Annual NOI | monthly NOI × 12 | $ _______ |
| Annual debt service | monthly P&I × 12 | $ _______ |
| Annual pre-tax cash flow | monthly cash flow × 12 | $ _______ |
| Total cash invested | cash to close (B1) | $ _______ |

### B4. Return Metrics — compute on every deal

| Metric | Formula | Target | Your number | Pass? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cap rate** | annual NOI ÷ purchase price | compare across properties (financing-independent) | _____ % | ☐ |
| **Cash-on-cash return** | annual pre-tax cash flow ÷ total cash invested | **positive**; ≥ 6–8% is healthy | _____ % | ☐ |
| **DSCR** | annual NOI ÷ annual debt service | lenders want **≥ 1.20–1.25** | _____ | ☐ |

**Read the metrics together:**
- **Cap rate** compares properties to each other and to the market, ignoring your loan.
- **Cash-on-cash** tells you whether *leverage is working* — a negative number means you're
  feeding the property monthly and betting purely on appreciation + loan paydown. That's
  speculation, not income investing.
- **DSCR** is the lender's safety test; below ~1.20 the loan is thin and a single bad month hurts.

### C-B4. Return Metrics

| Metric | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Cap rate | ~$18,240 annual NOI ÷ $240,000 | ≈ **7.6%** *(self-managed; ~6.0–6.5% if managed)* |
| Cash-on-cash | negative annual cash flow ÷ ~$66k–$69k invested | **negative** |
| DSCR | ~$18,240 NOI ÷ ~$14,736 debt service | ≈ **1.24** *(self-managed; falls below 1.20 once management is added)* |

**Verdict on the example deal:** **Do not buy it as priced.** It fails the 1% screen, the
cash-to-close exceeds your deployable savings, and cash-on-cash is negative — you'd be
subsidizing the property every month and betting entirely on appreciation.

> **Note on the numbers:** my Round 1 narrative quoted a wider negative range (≈ −$250 to −$550)
> by reserving maintenance/CapEx at the higher end and including a property-management line. This
> worksheet shows the *self-managed, mid-range* case (≈ −$60 to −$260). Both land in the same
> place: **negative cash flow before the tax picture**, and worse once you pay for management or
> hit a high-tax/high-insurance locale. The point of the worksheet is that *your verified local
> tax, insurance, and rent comps* decide where in that range a real deal lands — so fill them in.

## PART D — What Makes a Deal Actually Work

If a property fails Part A, look for one where the math flips:

1. **Buy below median** — a $190k–$210k home renting at $1,800–$1,900 dramatically improves the
   ratio. Target cosmetically dated, not structurally compromised, homes.
2. **Higher-rent submarkets** — proximity to hospitals (Priya's network), strong school zones,
   stable employers support both rent and tenant quality.
3. **Larger down payment** cuts debt service — but this collides with "don't drain the safety
   net." Trade-off owned by the **Financial Planner (01)**.
4. **Force appreciation / value-add** — light rehab that lifts rent, or a legal accessory unit.
   Higher complexity for a first-timer.
5. **Negotiate price** — at a 0.79% rent ratio you need price discipline. Make offers that
   pencil on *this worksheet*; walk from the ones that don't.
