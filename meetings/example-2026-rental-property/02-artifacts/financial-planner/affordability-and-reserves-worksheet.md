## How to use this worksheet

This is a reusable decision tool. The numbers below are filled in with your **brief figures** so you
can see how the test works on your actual situation. Every input row has a **"Your figure"** column
(what we used) and a **"This property"** column you fill in when you have a specific deal under
consideration. Run the worksheet against any property before making an offer.

There are four parts, and a deal must pass **all four**:

1. **Part A — Cash to close vs. available cash** (do you have enough to buy at all?)
2. **Part B — Post-closing reserve target** (do you have a cushion *after* you buy?)
3. **Part C — The "leaves ≥ $25k liquid" test** (the hard floor your safety net must clear)
4. **Part D — The 3-hit stress test** (can the household survive vacancy + repair + leave at once?)

A property that fails any part is the wrong deal **for your balance sheet** — independent of how good
the property itself looks. Deal economics (rent comps, vacancy %, cash flow) are the Real Estate
Advisor's (06) call; this worksheet governs whether *your household* can absorb the purchase.

**Question A answers:** Can you fund the purchase *without* touching the emergency fund?

**Result for the $240k example:** **FAIL.** Cash to close (~$62k) exceeds available cash (~$47k) by
roughly **$15,000** *before any reserve is set aside.* Buying at $240k / 20% down would require either
touching the emergency fund (off-limits) or finding ~$15k more cash.

### A-supplement: What price point *does* fit $47k?

Use this to back into an affordable price. Solving so that **cash to close ≤ ~$40,000** (leaving ~$7k of
the $47k toward reserves):

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $240,000 | $48,000 | $8,000 | $5,000 | ~$61,000 | No (short ~$14k) |

> Takeaway: even at ~$170–185k, $47k covers *cash to close* but barely funds the post-closing reserve in

## Part C — The "leaves ≥ $25k liquid" test

**Question C answers:** After closing, do your combined safety reserves clear the hard $25k floor set in
Round 1?

The $25k floor = personal emergency fund **+** property reserve remaining liquid after the purchase. It is
the guardrail that keeps a household-balance-sheet "yes" from becoming an overextension.

| Line | Input | Example ($240k case) | This property |
|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | Total cash before purchase | ~$62,000 | __________ |
| C2 | Cash to close (from A6) | ~$62,000 | __________ |
| **C3** | **Liquid cash remaining = C1 − C2** | **~$0** | __________ |
| C4 | Required floor | $25,000 | $25,000 |
| **C5** | **Pass if C3 ≥ C4** | **FAIL (~$0 < $25k)** | __________ |

**Result for the $240k example:** **FAIL** by a wide margin — a $240k / 20%-down purchase would leave
essentially nothing liquid. To pass, the combination of (price point + down payment + cash on hand) must
leave at least **$25,000** sitting in your emergency fund and property reserve *after* you close.

### Worked example that PASSES (illustrative target)

If you (a) save ~$13k more (total cash ~$75k) **and** (b) target a ~$200k property:

| Line | Value |
|---|---|
| Total cash before purchase | ~$75,000 |
| Cash to close on ~$200k (down $40k + closing $7.5k + make-ready $5k) | ~$52,500 |
| Liquid cash remaining | ~$22,500 |
| Plus: keep emergency fund intact ($15k already excluded above) — note structure |  |

> The cleanest passing structures from your starting point are: **lower the price**, **raise cash over
> 6–12 months**, or **both**. Re-run Parts A–C against any specific deal until C5 reads **PASS** with the
> emergency fund untouched.

## Part D — The 3-hit stress test

**Question D answers:** If three bad things happen *at the same time*, can the household absorb them
**without touching the $15k emergency fund**?

Model these three simultaneous hits in the first 12 months of ownership:

| Hit | Definition | Example cost | This property |
|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | **Vacancy** — 2 months with no rent (you still pay full carry) | 2 × ~$1,660 carry = ~$3,320 | _____ × ______ = ______ |
| D2 | **Major repair** — one unplanned capex event | ~$5,000 | __________ |
| D3 | **Parental leave** — one of you out, reduced household income | See below | __________ |
| **D4** | **Total shock = D1 + D2 + D3 (net income gap)** | see total | __________ |

**Sizing D3 (parental leave):** if Priya (~$85k ≈ ~$7,080/mo gross) takes 8 weeks at, say, 50% pay, the
income gap ≈ ~$7,080 × 0.5 × 2 months ≈ **~$7,080** *(illustrative — depends on employer leave policy and
state benefits; verify your actual leave terms)*.

| Line | Stress total |
|---|---|
| D1 Vacancy | ~$3,320 |
| D2 Repair | ~$5,000 |
| D3 Leave income gap | ~$7,080 |
| **D4 Combined 3-hit shock** | **~$15,400** |

**The test:** Can the **property reserve (Part B) + remaining liquid cash (Part C, line C3)** absorb the
~$15,400 shock — *without* drawing on the $15k personal emergency fund?

| Line | Input | Example ($240k case) | This property |
|---|---|---|---|
| D5 | Property reserve available (B9) | ~$15,000 | __________ |
| D6 | Other liquid cash remaining (C3) | ~$0 | __________ |
| **D7** | **Shock-absorbing capacity = D5 + D6** | **~$15,000** | __________ |
| D8 | Combined 3-hit shock (D4) | ~$15,400 | __________ |
| **D9** | **Pass if D7 ≥ D8** | **FAIL (~$15.0k < ~$15.4k)** | __________ |

**Result for the $240k example:** **FAIL** — and note *why* it fails: in that scenario you had to spend
nearly all your cash just to close (C3 ≈ $0), so the property reserve alone has to absorb the entire
shock and comes up short. A right-sized purchase that leaves the ≥$25k liquid floor (Part C) intact
passes this comfortably, because D6 is no longer zero.

> The parental-leave leg (D3) is the one most specific to you: with a baby planned "within a few years"
> and the larger income on Priya's side, an income dip is not a tail risk — it's a **planned event**. Build
> the reserve to survive it *on purpose*, not by luck.
