## Verdict

**The $240k @ $1,900 deal is a pass** — confirmed independently by both advisors:

- **Real Estate (the property):** fails the 1% rule (0.79%) and runs ~−$60 to −$260/month self-managed —
  you'd feed it monthly and bet on appreciation. Cash-to-close (~$66k–$69k at 25% down) already exceeds the
  ~$47k available.
- **Financial Planner (the balance sheet):** even at 20% down, cash-to-close (~$57k–$68k) exceeds available
  cash before reserves; the purchase only "fits" by raiding the emergency fund, which is off-limits.

When the deal analysis and the balance-sheet analysis fail at once, the answer isn't clever financing — it's
a different, smaller target.

## What a "yes" looks like

- **Target ~$185k–$210k**, with rent verified near ~$1,800–$1,900 by **signed-lease comps** (not asking
  rents or seller pro-formas).
- **Raise total cash to ~$70k** (~$15k–$20k more) over the next 6–12 months.
- **The $15k emergency fund is sacred** — it funds no part of any purchase. Stand up a **separate property
  reserve** (~$15k) before possession. Two pots, two jobs.
- **Time the purchase around — not into — a parental-leave year.**

   household can absorb the purchase.*

**Make an offer only if worksheet 1 and worksheet 3 both pass on the same property.**

## Key tension, resolved

Appreciation thesis (Real Estate) vs. strict cash-flow discipline (Financial Planner) resolves in favor of
discipline: an appreciation bet only pays if you can hold through the bad year — vacancy, a roof, parental
leave — and **reserves are precisely what keep that bet alive.** Buy in a credible-appreciation neighborhood,
but only at a price and reserve structure that clears all four affordability tests.
