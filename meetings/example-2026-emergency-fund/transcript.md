## Financial Planner (01) — convened

**Chair → Financial Planner:** Daniel wants a recommended emergency-fund target sized to his situation, a specific place to hold it, and a build plan from where he is. Educate him a little — he's relatively new to this.

**Financial Planner:** Daniel, the short answer first, then the reasoning.

### The recommendation

**Target: 4 months of essential expenses, which for you is about $12,800** (4 × $3,200). Hold it in a **dedicated high-yield savings account (HYSA)** at an FDIC-insured institution, separate from your everyday checking. That's safety, same-day-to-next-day access, real yield, and zero maintenance.

You're not underprepared today — you're **under-organized**. Your $6,000 is doing emergency-fund duty *and* general-savings duty at once, and it's sitting in a low-yield account. We're going to fix both.

### Why 4 months — not 3, not 6

The standard rule of thumb is 3–6 months of essential expenses. Where you land inside that range depends on how stable and replaceable your income is. Your profile:

| Factor | Your situation | Pushes the target… |
|---|---|---|
| Income type | Single W-2 salary, paid twice monthly | Toward lower end (predictable) |
| Job security | Stable employer, role feels secure | Toward lower end |
| Dependents | Single, no dependents | Toward lower end |
| Income sources | One income, no second earner | Toward higher end |
| High-interest debt | None | Toward lower end |
| Housing | Renter — no surprise roof/HVAC | Toward lower end |

Most factors point low. The one that doesn't is the **single-income reality** — if your paycheck stops, there is no backup. That's what keeps you off the 3-month floor. **4 months is the balanced answer.** Enough to absorb a job loss plus a typical 2–4 month search for a comparable role, without forcing high-interest debt or an early 401(k) withdrawal — and without parking so much idle cash that you sacrifice long-term growth.

**One framing point that matters:** size the fund on **essential expenses ($3,200), not take-home pay ($4,300)**. In a real emergency you cut discretionary spending. That's why the target is $12,800 and not $17,200.

### Your "too much idle cash" instinct

You asked about the trade-off, and the instinct is right:

| Risk | What it costs you |
|---|---|
| **Too little** cash | A single shock (job loss, medical bill, car repair) forces credit-card debt at 20%+ or an early 401(k) withdrawal — taxes + a 10% penalty + lost compounding. Expensive and hard to undo. |
| **Too much** cash | Every dollar above your target earns a modest savings yield instead of long-term market returns. Over decades, a real opportunity cost — but only on the *excess*. |

The asymmetry matters: being underprepared can cost you thousands now; being over-cushioned costs slower growth on the excess only. So we fund the target deliberately, then **stop** — once you hit ~$12,800, additional savings flow to higher-return goals. That's the discipline that keeps you from the "too much idle" trap.

### Where to park it

You want low-maintenance, low-risk, accessible, not idle. Best fit first:

| Option | Safety | Access | Yield vs. idle cash | Fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **High-yield savings (HYSA)** | FDIC-insured to $250k *(illustrative — verify current-year value)* | 1–2 business days | Many multiples of a big-bank rate | **Best fit** |
| Money market fund (at a brokerage) | Very high; not FDIC-insured | 1–2 business days | Comparable to HYSA | Good alternative |
| CD ladder | FDIC-insured | Locked until maturity (penalty to break) | Slightly higher, rigid | Poor — defeats "accessible" |
| Checking / regular savings (today) | FDIC-insured | Instant | Near zero — this is the "idle" problem | What we're moving away from |

A **dedicated HYSA at an institution separate from your checking** wins on two counts: (1) **yield without effort** — many multiples of a big-bank savings rate, so the money stops being idle without you having to manage anything; (2) **friction is a feature** — keeping it one transfer away makes it less tempting to dip into for non-emergencies while still being available within a day or two. I don't endorse named institutions — compare current FDIC-insured HYSA rates when you open it.

### How to build it from where you are

You're closer than you think. Treat the $6,000 as the seed, not zero.

1. **Open a dedicated HYSA** at an FDIC-insured institution separate from your bank. Name the account "Emergency Fund" — give it one job.
2. **Move $5,000 of your $6,000 into it now.** Keep ~$1,000 in checking as a cash-flow buffer. Starting balance: **$5,000 of a $12,800 target.**
3. **Automate the gap.** Your monthly surplus is roughly $1,100 ($4,300 take-home − $3,200 essentials). Direct a fixed amount to the HYSA by automatic transfer on payday, before you can spend it.
   - At **~$650/month**, you reach $12,800 in about **12 months**.
   - At **~$900/month**, you get there in about **8–9 months**.
   - Either pace works. I'd lean ~$650–700 so you keep budget breathing room.
4. **Stop at the target.** Once the HYSA hits ~$12,800, cancel the automatic transfer. Don't let it keep growing — that's the leak you asked about.
5. **Redeploy the freed cash flow.** After the fund is full, that ~$1,100/month is your single most valuable resource — and it should flow to higher-return goals. Priority order: keep capturing the full 401(k) match (already doing it — that's the highest-priority dollar in your budget), then a Roth IRA is a strong fit at your age and likely tax bracket *(contribution limit is year-specific — illustrative; verify current-year value)*, then the student loan if its rate is high relative to expected after-tax investment returns.

### Two things adjacent to your question — flagging for later

You asked a focused question and I've answered it. Two items worth keeping on your radar — they don't change today's plan:

- **Student loan vs. invest the surplus.** Once the fund is built, whether to accelerate payoff or invest depends on the loan's rate vs. your expected after-tax investment return. The Tax Strategist (02) can weigh in on whether the student-loan interest deduction applies to you, which shifts the math.
- **You're already doing the most important thing right.** Full 401(k) match captured. That's free money and it stays priority #1 above everything except the emergency fund itself.

You're in a genuinely solid position for 29: no high-interest debt, match captured, positive monthly cash flow. The emergency fund is the one missing foundation stone, and you can lay it in under a year.
