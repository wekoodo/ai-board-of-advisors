# Financial Planner — Initial Meeting

Daniel, here is the short answer, then the reasoning behind it.

## The Recommendation

**Target: 4 months of essential expenses, which for you is about $12,800** (4 × $3,200). Build it in stages, and **keep it in a high-yield savings account (HYSA) at an FDIC-insured bank** — separate from your everyday checking. That gives you safety, same-day-to-next-day access, and a real yield, without you having to manage anything.

You are not underprepared today, but you are under-organized: your $6,000 is doing emergency-fund duty *and* general-savings duty at once, and it is sitting in a low-yield account. We will fix both.

## Why 4 Months Is Right For You

The standard rule of thumb is 3–6 months of essential expenses. That range exists because the right number depends on how stable and replaceable your income is. Here is how your profile maps onto it:

| Factor | Your situation | Pushes the target… |
|---|---|---|
| Income type | Single W-2 salary, paid twice monthly | Toward lower end (predictable) |
| Job security | Stable employer, role feels secure | Toward lower end |
| Dependents | Single, no dependents | Toward lower end |
| Income sources | One income, no second earner to fall back on | Toward higher end |
| High-interest debt | None | Toward lower end |
| Housing | Renter (no surprise roof/HVAC repairs) | Toward lower end |

Most factors point low, but the single-income reality keeps you off the rock-bottom 3-month figure — if your one paycheck stops, there is no backup. **4 months is the balanced choice.** It is enough to absorb a job loss plus a job search (the average search for a stable role often runs 2–4 months) without forcing you to raid retirement or take on debt, and without parking so much idle cash that you sacrifice long-term growth.

**Important framing on the number:** size the fund on **essential expenses ($3,200), not take-home pay ($4,300)**. In a genuine emergency you would cut discretionary spending, so $3,200/month is the figure that actually has to be covered. This is why your target is $12,800 and not $17,200.

### The "too much idle cash" trade-off you asked about

This is the right instinct to have. Here is the tension:

| Risk | What it costs you |
|---|---|
| **Too little** cash | A single shock (job loss, medical bill, car repair) forces high-interest credit-card debt or an early 401(k) withdrawal — taxes + a 10% penalty + lost compounding. Expensive and hard to undo. |
| **Too much** cash | Every dollar above your target earns a modest savings yield instead of long-term market returns. Over decades, that gap is real "opportunity cost." |

The asymmetry matters: being underprepared can cost you thousands in penalties and interest *now*; being over-cushioned costs you a slower-growing return on the *excess* only. So we fund the target deliberately, then **stop** — once you hit ~$12,800, additional savings flow to higher-return goals rather than piling up in cash. That is the discipline that keeps you from the "too much idle" trap. For where those next dollars should go, the Investment Advisor (05) can map the allocation.

## Where To Park It

You want low-maintenance, low-risk, accessible, and not idle. Here are the realistic homes, best fit first:

| Option | Safety | Access | Yield vs. idle cash | Maintenance | Fit for you |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **High-yield savings account (HYSA)** | FDIC-insured to $250k *(illustrative — verify current-year value)* | 1–2 business days | Meaningfully higher than a big-bank savings account | None — set and forget | **Best fit** |
| Money market fund (at a brokerage) | Very high, not FDIC-insured | 1–2 business days | Comparable to HYSA | None | Good alternative |
| CD ladder | FDIC-insured | Locked until maturity (penalty to break) | Slightly higher, but rigid | Low | Poor — defeats "accessible" |
| Checking/regular savings (today) | FDIC-insured | Instant | Near zero — this is the "idle" problem | None | What we're moving away from |

**Recommendation: a dedicated HYSA, held at a different institution from your checking.** Two reasons it works for you specifically:
1. **Yield without effort.** A HYSA typically pays many multiples of a standard big-bank savings rate, so your emergency fund stops being idle — it just isn't *invested*, which is correct for money you must not risk.
2. **Friction is a feature.** Keeping it one transfer away (not instantly spendable from your checking) makes it less tempting to dip into for non-emergencies, while still being available within a day or two when you truly need it.

Specific products and rates change constantly and I don't endorse named institutions — compare current FDIC-insured HYSA rates when you open it, and confirm the yield is current.

## How To Build It From Where You Are

You are closer than you think. Of your $6,000, treat it as the seed of the fund rather than starting from zero.

1. **Open a dedicated HYSA** at an FDIC-insured institution separate from your bank. Name the account "Emergency Fund" so it has one job.
2. **Move $5,000 of your existing $6,000 into it now.** Keep ~$1,000 as a buffer in checking for ordinary cash-flow timing. This instantly gets your fund earning real yield. Starting balance: **$5,000 of a $12,800 target.**
3. **Automate the gap.** You have roughly **$1,100/month of surplus** ($4,300 take-home − $3,200 essentials). Direct a fixed amount to the HYSA by automatic transfer on payday so it happens before you can spend it.
   - At **$650/month**, you reach $12,800 in about **12 months**.
   - At **$900/month**, you get there in about **8–9 months**.
   - Either pace still leaves margin in your budget. I'd lean toward ~$650–700/month so you keep breathing room and don't feel squeezed.
4. **Stop at the target.** Once the HYSA hits ~$12,800, cancel or redirect the automatic transfer. Don't let it keep growing — that's the "too much idle" leak.
5. **Redeploy the freed-up cash flow.** After the fund is full, that ~$1,100/month is your single most valuable resource. In priority order it goes to: the student loan (if its rate is high relative to expected investment returns — worth a closer look), then tax-advantaged retirement beyond the match (a Roth IRA is a strong fit at your age and likely tax bracket). More on that below.
